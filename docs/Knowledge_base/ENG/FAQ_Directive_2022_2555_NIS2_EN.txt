FAQ - DIRECTIVE (EU) 2022/2555 - CYBERSECURITY (NIS 2 DIRECTIVE)
========================================================================


--- SECTION 1: WHAT IS THE DIRECTIVE AND WHY DOES IT EXIST ---

Q: What is the main objective of Directive (EU) 2022/2555?
A: The Directive aims to achieve a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union, with a
   view to improving the functioning of the internal market. It lays down obligations for Member
   States to adopt national cybersecurity strategies, cybersecurity risk-management measures
   and reporting obligations for essential and important entities, rules on information sharing,
   and supervisory and enforcement obligations.

Q: What prompted the EU to adopt this new Directive?
A: The previous Directive (EU) 2016/1148 (NIS1) had inherent shortcomings that prevented
   effective response to current and emerging cybersecurity challenges. Its review revealed wide
   divergences in implementation across Member States — particularly regarding scope, security
   obligations, incident reporting, supervision and enforcement — which fragmented the
   internal market and created uneven levels of cyber resilience across the Union.

Q: Does this Directive repeal any previous legislation?
A: Yes. Directive (EU) 2022/2555 repeals Directive (EU) 2016/1148 with effect from
   18 October 2024. It also deletes Article 19 of Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 and Articles 40
   and 41 of Directive (EU) 2018/1972 with effect from the same date.


--- SECTION 2: KEY DEFINITIONS ---

Q: What is an 'incident' under this Directive?
A: An event compromising the availability, authenticity, integrity or confidentiality of stored,
   transmitted or processed data, or of the services offered by, or accessible via, network and
   information systems.

Q: What is a 'significant incident'?
A: An incident that has caused or is capable of causing severe operational disruption of services
   or financial loss for the entity concerned, or that has affected or is capable of affecting other
   natural or legal persons by causing considerable material or non-material damage.

Q: What is a 'large-scale cybersecurity incident'?
A: An incident which causes a level of disruption that exceeds a Member State's capacity to
   respond to it, or which has a significant impact on at least two Member States.

Q: What is a 'cyber threat'?
A: Any potential circumstance, event or action that could damage, disrupt or adversely affect
   network and information systems, their users, or other persons (as defined by reference to
   Regulation (EU) 2019/881, the Cybersecurity Act).

Q: What is a 'vulnerability'?
A: A weakness, susceptibility or flaw of ICT products or ICT services that can be exploited by
   a cyber threat.

Q: How is 'risk' defined?
A: The potential for loss or disruption caused by an incident, expressed as a combination of
   the magnitude of such loss or disruption and the likelihood of occurrence of the incident.

Q: What is a 'near miss'?
A: An event that could have compromised the availability, authenticity, integrity or
   confidentiality of data or of services offered via network and information systems, but that
   was successfully prevented from materialising or that did not materialise.


--- SECTION 3: SECTORS AND ENTITIES IN SCOPE ---

Q: Which sectors fall within the scope of the Directive?
A: The Directive covers two tiers of sectors:
   Annex I — Sectors of high criticality (11 sectors):
    1.  Energy
    2.  Transport
    3.  Banking
    4.  Financial market infrastructures
    5.  Health
    6.  Drinking water
    7.  Waste water
    8.  Digital infrastructure
    9.  ICT service management (business-to-business)
    10. Public administration
    11. Space
   Annex II — Other critical sectors (7 sectors):
    1.  Postal and courier services
    2.  Waste management
    3.  Manufacture, production and distribution of chemicals
    4.  Production, processing and distribution of food
    5.  Manufacturing (medical devices, electronics, machinery, vehicles, etc.)
    6.  Digital providers (online marketplaces, search engines, social networks)
    7.  Research

Q: What is the size threshold for entities to fall within scope?
A: As a general rule, the Directive applies to entities that qualify as medium-sized enterprises
   (50 or more employees and annual turnover or balance sheet exceeding EUR 10 million) or
   exceed those ceilings. Certain categories of entities are included regardless of size, such as
   trust service providers, TLD name registries, DNS service providers, providers of public
   electronic communications networks, and sole providers of essential services in a Member
   State.

Q: What is the difference between 'essential entities' and 'important entities'?
A: Essential entities are large entities in Annex I sectors, as well as specific categories
   regardless of size (qualified trust service providers, TLD registries, DNS service providers,
   providers of public electronic communications networks, central government public
   administration entities, and entities identified as critical under Directive (EU) 2022/2557).
   Important entities are all other entities falling within scope that do not qualify as essential.
   The distinction affects the supervisory regime: essential entities are subject to comprehensive
   ex ante and ex post supervision; important entities are subject to a lighter, ex post only regime.

Q: Who is excluded from the scope of the Directive?
A: Public administration entities carrying out activities predominantly in the areas of national
   security, public security, defence or law enforcement are excluded. Member States may also
   exempt specific entities operating in those areas. The Directive does not apply to Member
   States' diplomatic and consular missions in third countries.

Q: Does the Directive cover physical resilience?
A: No. Physical resilience of critical entities is addressed by the CER Directive
   (EU) 2022/2557. NIS2 focuses on cybersecurity. The two Directives are complementary:
   entities identified as critical under the CER Directive are automatically treated as essential
   entities under NIS2, and competent authorities under both Directives must cooperate and
   exchange information.

Q: Are financial entities subject to full NIS2 obligations?
A: No. Financial entities covered by Regulation (EU) 2022/2554 (DORA) are not subject to
   the cybersecurity risk-management and reporting obligations under NIS2, as DORA provides
   equivalent sector-specific requirements. However, information exchange between the
   financial sector and NIS2 authorities is maintained.


--- SECTION 4: OBLIGATIONS OF MEMBER STATES ---

Q: What must Member States do to transpose the Directive?
A: Member States must adopt and publish the necessary national measures by 17 October 2024,
   with application from 18 October 2024. By 17 April 2025, they must also establish a list of
   essential and important entities, reviewed at least every two years thereafter.

Q: What must the national cybersecurity strategy contain?
A: At least: strategic objectives and priorities covering Annex I and II sectors; a governance
   framework; a framework clarifying roles and responsibilities of stakeholders; a mechanism
   to identify assets and assess risks; measures for preparedness, responsiveness and recovery;
   a list of authorities involved; a policy framework for coordination with CER authorities; and
   a plan to raise cybersecurity awareness among citizens.

Q: What governance bodies must Member States designate or establish?
A: Each Member State must designate or establish: one or more competent authorities for
   cybersecurity supervision; a single point of contact for cross-border cooperation; one or more
   cyber crisis management authorities for large-scale incidents; and one or more CSIRTs
   (Computer Security Incident Response Teams).

Q: How often must the list of essential and important entities be reviewed?
A: At least every two years. By 17 April 2025, Member States must notify the Commission of
   the number of essential and important entities per sector, updating this information every
   two years thereafter.

Q: Can Member States apply stricter national rules?
A: Yes. The Directive establishes minimum harmonisation. Member States may adopt or
   maintain provisions ensuring a higher level of cybersecurity, provided they are consistent
   with Union law.


--- SECTION 5: OBLIGATIONS OF ESSENTIAL AND IMPORTANT ENTITIES ---

Q: What cybersecurity risk-management measures must entities take?
A: Appropriate and proportionate technical, operational and organisational measures based on
   an all-hazards approach, including at least:
   - Risk analysis and information system security policies;
   - Incident handling procedures;
   - Business continuity, backup management, disaster recovery and crisis management;
   - Supply chain security, including direct supplier and service provider relationships;
   - Security in network and information systems acquisition, development and maintenance,
     including vulnerability handling and disclosure;
   - Policies to assess the effectiveness of cybersecurity measures;
   - Basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training;
   - Policies on the use of cryptography and, where appropriate, encryption;
   - Human resources security, access control policies and asset management;
   - Multi-factor authentication or continuous authentication solutions, where appropriate.

Q: Who is responsible for approving and overseeing cybersecurity measures within an entity?
A: The management body of the entity must approve the cybersecurity risk-management
   measures, oversee their implementation and can be held liable for infringements. Management
   body members are required to follow cybersecurity training; entities are encouraged to offer
   similar training to all employees.

Q: Can existing measures satisfy NIS2 requirements?
A: Yes. Where sector-specific Union legal acts require equivalent cybersecurity risk-management
   measures or reporting obligations, those provisions apply instead of the corresponding NIS2
   obligations. Entities subject to DORA or equivalent sectoral regulation may already meet
   their obligations under those instruments.

Q: Are entities required to address supply chain risks?
A: Yes. Entities must assess the vulnerabilities specific to each direct supplier and service
   provider, and consider the overall quality of products and cybersecurity practices throughout
   their supply chains. They must also take into account the results of coordinated security risk
   assessments of critical supply chains carried out at Union level by the Cooperation Group.


--- SECTION 6: INCIDENT REPORTING OBLIGATIONS ---

Q: When must an entity report a significant incident?
A: Without undue delay, following a multi-stage approach:
   - Within 24 hours of becoming aware: an early warning, indicating if the incident is
     suspected to be caused by malicious acts or likely to have cross-border impact;
   - Within 72 hours of becoming aware: an incident notification with an initial assessment,
     severity and impact, and indicators of compromise where available;
   - Upon request: intermediate status reports;
   - Within one month of the incident notification: a final report with detailed description,
     root cause, mitigation measures applied and cross-border impact.
   For trust service providers, the initial notification deadline is 24 hours (not 72 hours).

Q: Must entities also inform service recipients?
A: Yes, where appropriate. Entities must communicate without undue delay to potentially
   affected service recipients any significant cyber threats and the measures or remedies they
   can take. Where the threat is likely to materialise, entities should also inform recipients of
   the threat itself. Such communications must be free of charge and in clear language.

Q: What happens after the early warning is received?
A: The CSIRT or competent authority must provide initial feedback to the notifying entity,
   including guidance on possible mitigation measures, within 24 hours of receiving the early
   warning. Additional technical support is available upon request. If the incident is suspected
   to be of criminal nature, guidance on reporting to law enforcement will also be provided.

Q: What if an incident affects multiple Member States?
A: The CSIRT, competent authority or single point of contact must inform the other affected
   Member States and ENISA without undue delay. The single point of contact submits to ENISA
   every three months a summary report of aggregated and anonymised data on significant
   incidents, threats and near misses notified.


--- SECTION 7: GOVERNANCE AND COORDINATION ---

Q: What is the 'Cooperation Group'?
A: A Commission expert group composed of representatives of Member States, the Commission
   and ENISA, established to support strategic cooperation and exchange of information among
   Member States. It carries out tasks including: providing guidance on transposition; exchanging
   best practices; conducting coordinated security risk assessments of critical supply chains;
   and producing biennial work programmes. It meets at least once a year with the Critical
   Entities Resilience Group established under the CER Directive.

Q: What is the 'CSIRTs network'?
A: A network of national CSIRTs and CERT-EU, with ENISA as secretariat, established to
   develop confidence and trust and promote swift operational cooperation. Its tasks include
   exchanging information on threats, vulnerabilities and incidents; providing mutual assistance;
   coordinating responses to cross-border incidents; and supporting coordinated vulnerability
   disclosure.

Q: What is EU-CyCLONe?
A: The European Cyber Crisis Liaison Organisation Network, composed of Member States'
   cyber crisis management authorities and the Commission. It supports the coordinated
   management of large-scale cybersecurity incidents and crises at operational level, acts as
   intermediary between technical and political levels, and reports every 18 months to the
   European Parliament and the Council.

Q: How does this Directive coordinate with the CER Directive?
A: The two Directives are complementary. Entities identified as critical under the CER Directive
   are treated as essential entities under NIS2. Competent authorities under both Directives
   must cooperate regularly on identification of critical entities, cyber and non-cyber risks,
   incidents and supervisory activities. They must endeavour to harmonise notification templates
   and supervisory processes to minimise administrative burden.

Q: What is the role of ENISA under this Directive?
A: ENISA provides the secretariat of the CSIRTs network and EU-CyCLONe, maintains the
   European vulnerability database, creates and maintains a registry of certain cross-border
   entities, develops guidelines and technical guidance, assists Member States in developing
   CSIRTs, publishes a biennial report on the state of cybersecurity in the Union, and supports
   the coordinated vulnerability disclosure framework.


--- SECTION 8: VULNERABILITY MANAGEMENT AND INFORMATION SHARING ---

Q: What is 'coordinated vulnerability disclosure'?
A: A structured process through which vulnerabilities in ICT products or services are reported
   to the manufacturer or provider in a manner allowing diagnosis and remediation before
   public disclosure. Each Member State must designate one of its CSIRTs as coordinator,
   acting as a trusted intermediary between the reporting party and the affected manufacturer
   or provider.

Q: What is the European vulnerability database?
A: A database developed and maintained by ENISA, allowing entities and their suppliers to
   voluntarily disclose and register publicly known vulnerabilities in ICT products or ICT
   services. It includes information describing the vulnerability, affected products and severity,
   and guidance on available patches or mitigating measures. All stakeholders have access.

Q: Can entities share cybersecurity information voluntarily?
A: Yes. Member States must ensure that entities — including those outside the scope of the
   Directive — can voluntarily exchange relevant cybersecurity information, such as threat
   intelligence, vulnerabilities, indicators of compromise and response recommendations,
   within cybersecurity information-sharing arrangements. Such voluntary reporting shall not
   result in additional obligations on the notifying entity.


--- SECTION 9: SUPERVISION, ENFORCEMENT AND PENALTIES ---

Q: How does supervision differ between essential and important entities?
A: Essential entities are subject to comprehensive ex ante and ex post supervision, including
   on-site inspections, regular and targeted security audits, security scans and information
   requests. Important entities are subject to a lighter ex post supervisory regime: supervision
   is triggered by evidence of potential non-compliance, not conducted proactively.

Q: What enforcement powers do competent authorities have?
A: Authorities may issue warnings, adopt binding instructions to remedy deficiencies, order
   cessation of infringing conduct, designate a monitoring officer, and impose administrative
   fines. As a last resort, for essential entities, they may also request temporary suspension of
   certifications or authorisations, or a temporary prohibition on a senior manager exercising
   managerial functions.

Q: What are the maximum administrative fines?
A: For essential entities: at least EUR 10 000 000 or at least 2 % of the total worldwide annual
   turnover of the undertaking concerned, whichever is higher.
   For important entities: at least EUR 7 000 000 or at least 1.4 % of the total worldwide annual
   turnover of the undertaking concerned, whichever is higher.

Q: What rights do entities have during supervisory proceedings?
A: The right to be heard, the right of defence, the right to protection of trade and business
   secrets, and the right to an effective remedy before an independent court. Competent
   authorities must state detailed reasoning for their enforcement measures and notify entities
   of preliminary findings, allowing a reasonable time for observations.

Q: When will the Commission carry out its first review of the Directive?
A: By 17 October 2027, and every 36 months thereafter, the Commission must review the
   functioning of the Directive and report to the European Parliament and to the Council,
   assessing the relevance of entity sizes, sectors and types covered in light of cybersecurity
   developments.
