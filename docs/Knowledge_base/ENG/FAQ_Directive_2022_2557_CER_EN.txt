FAQ - DIRECTIVE (EU) 2022/2557 - RESILIENCE OF CRITICAL ENTITIES (CER)
========================================================================


--- SECTION 1: WHAT IS THE DIRECTIVE AND WHY DOES IT EXIST ---

Q: What is the main objective of Directive (EU) 2022/2557?
A: The Directive aims to enhance the resilience of critical entities in the EU internal market.
   It lays down harmonised minimum rules to ensure the uninterrupted provision of essential
   services, supports critical entities through dedicated supervision and assistance measures,
   and improves cross-border cooperation between Member States.

Q: What prompted the EU to adopt this new Directive?
A: The previous Directive 2008/114/EC focused only on the protection of individual assets in
   two sectors (energy and transport). The 2019 evaluation found that hybrid threats, terrorism,
   climate change and growing cross-sectoral and cross-border interdependencies required a
   much broader, risk-based approach.

Q: Does this Directive repeal any previous legislation?
A: Yes. Directive 2022/2557 repeals Council Directive 2008/114/EC with effect from
   18 October 2024.


--- SECTION 2: KEY DEFINITIONS ---

Q: Who is a 'critical entity'?
A: A public or private entity identified by a Member State as belonging to one of the categories
   listed in the Annex to the Directive (e.g. energy suppliers, transport operators, healthcare
   providers, etc.).

Q: What does 'resilience' mean under this Directive?
A: Resilience is a critical entity's ability to prevent, protect against, respond to, resist,
   mitigate, absorb, accommodate and recover from an incident.

Q: What is an 'incident' under this Directive?
A: An event which has the potential to significantly disrupt, or that disrupts, the provision of
   an essential service — including when it affects the national systems that safeguard the rule
   of law.

Q: What is an 'essential service'?
A: A service which is crucial for the maintenance of vital societal functions, economic
   activities, public health and safety, or the environment.

Q: What is 'critical infrastructure'?
A: An asset, facility, equipment, network or system — or part thereof — which is necessary
   for the provision of an essential service.

Q: How is 'risk' defined?
A: The potential for loss or disruption caused by an incident, expressed as a combination of
   the magnitude of such loss or disruption and the likelihood of occurrence of the incident.


--- SECTION 3: SECTORS AND ENTITIES IN SCOPE ---

Q: Which sectors fall within the scope of the Directive?
A: The Annex lists 11 sectors:
    1.  Energy
    2.  Transport
    3.  Banking
    4.  Financial market infrastructure
    5.  Health
    6.  Drinking water
    7.  Waste water
    8.  Digital infrastructure
    9.  Public administration
    10. Space
    11. Production, processing and distribution of food

Q: Who is excluded from the scope of the Directive?
A: Public administration entities operating predominantly in the areas of national security,
   public security, defence or law enforcement are excluded. Member States may also decide
   not to apply part of the obligations to critical entities whose activities are predominantly
   related to those areas.

Q: Do banks and financial infrastructure entities have reduced obligations?
A: Yes. Critical entities in the banking (point 3), financial market infrastructure (point 4)
   and digital infrastructure (point 8) sectors are not subject to the obligations of Chapters
   III, IV and VI of the Directive, as they are already covered by equivalent sector-specific
   legislation (DORA, NIS2). However, they must still be identified by Member States, and
   Chapter II of the Directive applies to them.

Q: Does the Directive cover cybersecurity?
A: No. Cybersecurity is addressed by the NIS2 Directive (EU) 2022/2555. The CER Directive
   focuses on physical resilience. Member States must ensure both are implemented in a
   coordinated manner.


--- SECTION 4: OBLIGATIONS OF MEMBER STATES ---

Q: What must Member States do by 17 January 2026?
A: Each Member State must adopt a national strategy for enhancing the resilience of critical
   entities and complete its Member State risk assessment. By 17 July 2026, they must also
   identify the critical entities in the sectors listed in the Annex.

Q: What must the national strategy contain?
A: At least: strategic objectives and priorities; a governance framework; a description of
   measures and the risk assessment process; the process for identifying critical entities;
   support mechanisms (including for SMEs); and a coordination framework with NIS2
   competent authorities.

Q: How are critical entities identified?
A: A Member State identifies an entity as critical if it: provides one or more essential
   services; operates on its territory; and an incident would have a significant disruptive
   effect on the provision of those services. Criteria for assessing significance include the
   number of users affected, economic and social impact, market share, and the geographic
   area affected.

Q: How often must the list of critical entities be reviewed?
A: At least every four years, and whenever necessary.

Q: What information must Member States share with the Commission?
A: After identifying critical entities, each Member State must submit without undue delay:
   a list of essential services (if different from the Commission's list), the number of critical
   entities per sector and subsector, and any thresholds applied. This information must be
   updated at least every four years.


--- SECTION 5: OBLIGATIONS OF CRITICAL ENTITIES ---

Q: What must a critical entity do after being notified of its identification?
A: Within 9 months of notification, it must carry out its own risk assessment. Chapter III
   obligations (resilience measures) apply from 10 months after the notification. It must also
   designate a liaison officer as its point of contact with the competent authorities.

Q: What resilience measures must critical entities take?
A: Appropriate and proportionate technical, security and organisational measures to:
   - prevent incidents (including climate adaptation);
   - ensure adequate physical protection of premises and infrastructure;
   - respond to, resist and mitigate the consequences of incidents;
   - recover from incidents (business continuity, alternative supply chains);
   - ensure adequate employee security management (including background checks);
   - raise awareness among relevant personnel through training and exercises.

Q: What is a resilience plan and who must draw one up?
A: Every critical entity must have in place and apply a resilience plan (or equivalent
   documents) describing the measures it has taken. Documents already produced for other
   regulatory obligations may be reused if they cover the requirements of the Directive.

Q: How often must critical entities carry out their own risk assessment?
A: At least every four years, and whenever circumstances or risks change significantly.

Q: Can existing risk assessments or measures satisfy CER requirements?
A: Yes. Where a critical entity has already carried out risk assessments or taken measures
   pursuant to other legal obligations, it may use those to meet CER requirements. The
   competent authority may also formally declare existing assessments or measures as
   compliant, in whole or in part.


--- SECTION 6: INCIDENT NOTIFICATION ---

Q: When must a critical entity notify an incident?
A: Without undue delay, for incidents that significantly disrupt or have the potential to
   significantly disrupt the provision of essential services. An initial notification must be
   submitted no later than 24 hours after becoming aware of the incident, unless operationally
   unable to do so.

Q: What must the notification contain?
A: All available information necessary to enable the competent authority to understand the
   nature, cause and possible consequences of the incident, including any cross-border impact.
   Notification does not expose critical entities to increased liability.

Q: Is a follow-up report required after the initial notification?
A: Yes. Where relevant, a detailed report must be submitted no later than one month after
   the incident, complementing the initial notification with a more complete overview.

Q: What parameters are used to assess the significance of a disruption?
A: The number and proportion of users affected; the duration of the disruption; and the
   geographical area affected, including whether that area is geographically isolated.

Q: What happens if an incident impacts six or more Member States?
A: The competent authorities of the affected Member States must notify the Commission.
   The single point of contact also informs the single points of contact of the other
   Member States concerned.


--- SECTION 7: GOVERNANCE AND COORDINATION ---

Q: What is the 'single point of contact'?
A: An authority designated by each Member State to act as a liaison for cross-border
   cooperation. It coordinates communications with the single points of contact of other
   Member States and with the Critical Entities Resilience Group.

Q: What is the 'Critical Entities Resilience Group'?
A: A Commission expert group composed of Member State representatives that supports the
   Commission, facilitates cooperation between Member States and promotes the exchange of
   best practices. It meets at least once a year with the NIS2 Cooperation Group.

Q: How does this Directive coordinate with NIS2?
A: The two Directives are complementary: NIS2 covers cybersecurity, CER covers physical
   resilience. Competent authorities under both Directives must cooperate and exchange
   information. Member States must ensure coordinated implementation to avoid duplication
   and excessive burden on entities.

Q: Can Member States apply stricter national rules?
A: Yes. The Directive establishes minimum harmonisation. Member States may adopt or
   maintain national provisions to achieve a higher level of resilience, provided they are
   consistent with Union law.


--- SECTION 8: CRITICAL ENTITIES OF PARTICULAR EUROPEAN SIGNIFICANCE ---

Q: What is a critical entity of 'particular European significance'?
A: An entity already identified as critical that provides the same or similar essential services
   to or in six or more Member States and has been formally notified as such by the Commission.

Q: What additional obligations apply to these entities?
A: They must inform their competent authority that they provide services to six or more Member
   States. The Commission may then organise an advisory mission to assess their resilience
   measures. They must provide the advisory mission with access to all relevant information,
   systems and facilities.

Q: What are advisory missions?
A: Missions organised by the Commission to assess the resilience measures of critical entities
   of particular European significance. They are composed of experts from the relevant Member
   States and the Commission. The mission reports its findings within three months of
   completion. The Commission then issues an opinion on the entity's compliance.


--- SECTION 9: SUPERVISION, ENFORCEMENT AND PENALTIES ---

Q: How is compliance with obligations supervised?
A: National competent authorities have powers to conduct on-site inspections, off-site
   supervision, request information and evidence (including independent audits at the entity's
   expense), and issue remediation orders. These powers must be exercised in an objective,
   transparent and proportionate manner.

Q: Are penalties foreseen for non-compliant critical entities?
A: Yes. Member States must lay down rules on penalties that are effective, proportionate and
   dissuasive. These rules had to be notified to the Commission by 17 October 2024.

Q: When did Member States have to transpose the Directive?
A: By 17 October 2024. The national measures apply from 18 October 2024.

Q: When will the Commission carry out its first review of the Directive?
A: The Commission must submit a compliance assessment report to the European Parliament
   and the Council by 17 July 2027, and a broader review of the Directive's functioning
   by 17 June 2029.

Q: What rights do critical entities have during supervisory proceedings?
A: The right to be heard, the right of defence, the right to protection of trade and business
   secrets, and the right to an effective remedy before an independent court.
